Risk-Based Student Loan Pricing Model

Actuarial framework for federal loan pricing based on field-level default risk, institution quality, and progressive income adjustments

Author: Isaiah Kamrar | Data: College Scorecard (7,703 institutions, 24 fields) | Methods: Survival analysis, structural credit models, machine learning ensemble

Executive Summary

üéØ Core Proposition

Replace the current flat 5.50% federal student loan rate with risk-adjusted pricing based on academic major and institution quality, balanced by progressive income subsidies to protect low-income students. This hybrid model improves efficiency and equity simultaneously.

üí° Key Insight: Cross-Subsidies Are Hidden

Engineering majors (1.3% underemployment, $52,900 earnings) currently subsidize Philosophy majors (30% underemployment, $28,500 earnings) by ~0.65 percentage points. Both pay 5.50% regardless of risk. Low-income students are concentrated in high-risk fields and for-profit institutions, meaning the current "equal" system masks structural inequality.

Policy Analysis & Recommendations

üéØ Hybrid Model Formula

Interest_Rate = Base_Rate(5.00%) + Risk_Premium(0-0.75%) - Income_Subsidy(0.5-2.0%)

Base Rate: Reduced from 5.50% to 5.00% for all students
Risk Premium: Covers actuarial risk, capped at half of "fair" premium
Income Subsidy: Progressive adjustment based on family income

Addressing Key Concerns

Concern: "Discourages Liberal Arts"

Response: Income subsidies specifically protect low-income liberal arts students. Combined with expanded Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF), the model supports mission-driven careers while still providing price signals. Australia implemented similar pricing with minimal enrollment changes.

Concern: "Reinforces Inequality"

Response: Current system already has hidden inequality. Low-income students are concentrated at for-profit institutions with 24% default rates. Our model explicitly addresses SES via progressive subsidies, resulting in lower rates for all low-income students regardless of field.

Concern: "18-Year-Olds Can't Assess Risk"

Response: Agreed‚Äîtransparency is critical. Require mandatory disclosure of estimated monthly payments before enrollment, financial literacy training, and guardrails capping debt-to-expected-earnings at 1.5x.

Implementation Phases

Phase 1: Graduate Pilot (2026-2027)

  • MBA, JD, MD, and other professional programs
  • ~15% of annual loan volume ($20B)
  • Rationale: Older borrowers, clearer earnings data, higher debt
  • Lower political risk if pilot encounters issues

Phase 2: Undergraduate Expansion (2028-2030)

  • All undergraduate federal loans
  • Gradual premium introduction (0.25pp/year)
  • Monitor enrollment effects by field
  • Adjust based on Phase 1 learnings

Phase 3: Full Implementation (2031+)

  • Dynamic pricing updated annually
  • Integration with Income-Based Repayment (IBR) plans
  • Real-time risk dashboards for institutions
  • Institution accountability metrics tied to Title IV eligibility

Technical Methodology

Data Sources

  • College Scorecard (IPEDS): 7,703 institutions, earnings and completion data
  • Federal Student Aid: 3-year cohort default rates
  • BLS Occupational Employment Statistics: Employment and wage data
  • NCES Graduate Student Aid Survey: Graduate program debt and outcomes

Default Probability Model

Default_Prob = 0.15 √ó Underemployment_Rate + 0.05
Risk_Premium = (Default_Prob √ó LGD √ó 100) / Duration

Where:
- LGD (Loss Given Default) = 70% (30% recovery)
- Duration = 10 years (standard repayment)
- Base Rate = 5.50% (current undergraduate rate)

This structural approach estimates default risk using underemployment as a proxy for repayment difficulty. The 0.15 coefficient was calibrated against historical cohort default rates from Federal Student Aid data.

Institution Quality Adjustments

Risk multipliers based on Carnegie Classification, selectivity, and control:

  • R1 + Most Selective + Private: 0.40x (elite institutions)
  • R1 + Selective + Public: 0.70x (flagship universities)
  • Master's + Moderate + Public: 0.90x (regional state universities)
  • Bachelor's + Low + Public: 1.15x (smaller public colleges)
  • Associate + Open + Public: 1.32x (community colleges)
  • Any + Any + For-Profit: 2.18x (for-profit institutions)

Ensemble Model (Advanced)

Developed and tested multiple approaches:

  1. Survival Analysis: Cox proportional hazards for time-to-default
  2. Structural Credit Model: Merton-style distance-to-default
  3. Machine Learning: Random Forest with fairness constraints
  4. Actuarial Life Table: Traditional insurance approach

Recommendation: Use simple structural model for production. Ensemble adds complexity without material improvement in predictive power for policy applications. Reserve ML for precision intervention targeting (see separate ML project).
