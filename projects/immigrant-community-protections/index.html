Oregon's Reality: Fear as a Daily Companion

Recent Oregon Incidents

  • Newport (2025): ICE agents in unmarked vehicles conducted public arrest. Restaurant workers too afraid to come to work the next day, forcing business closures.
  • Beaverton: Father arrested outside child's school, traumatizing entire community.
  • Farmworkers: Picked up on their way to blueberry fields, disrupting agricultural workforce.
  • Washington County Emergency: County declared state of emergency due to ICE enforcement surge.

The Chilling Effect

County commissioner likened ICE's fear-inducing tactics to "acts of terror against the community." Residents described "fear as a daily companion" that kept many from leaving homes, missing work, school, and medical appointments.

Understanding Risk Pathways: From Services to Surveillance

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL FINDING

Federal immigration enforcement operates primarily through fear and surveillance concerns rather than systematic data sharing. However, Trump-era policies created unprecedented digital risk pathways that turned service programs into potential enforcement tools.

The Mechanism: How Data Becomes Danger

Step 1: Data Collection

Immigrant Seeks Help

Applies for energy assistance, housing aid, healthcare, or food benefits. Provides name, address, household composition, SSN (for eligible family members), immigration status documentation.

Step 2: Database Entry

Information Stored in Government Systems

Personal identifying information (PII) entered into state/federal databases for eligibility verification and program administration.

Step 3: Cross-Agency Access

The "Vast Web of Data"

ICE gains access through: inter-agency agreements, SAVE system, Nlets network, data broker purchases, subpoenas, or AI-powered data mining (Palantir's ImmigrationOS).

Step 4: Enforcement Action

Location, Identification, Apprehension

ICE uses gathered data to track individuals, verify addresses, coordinate raids, and execute deportations.

Programs at Risk: Federal Funding = Exposure

Energy Assistance Programs (LIHEAP)

‚ñº
Data Risk Federal Program

Data Collection Requirements

  • Non-citizen applicants must submit immigration status documentation
  • Social Security numbers required for verification
  • Home addresses for utility account matching
  • Household composition details
Risk Level: MEDIUM-HIGH

While LIHEAP has some privacy protections, the federal funding connection and address data collection create potential exposure. Immigrants fear utility records could be cross-referenced by ICE.

Oregon-Specific Protections

Oregon Energy Assistance Program (OEAP): Governor Kotek and legislature doubled state funding to $40 million (2023-2025). State-controlled funds offer opportunity for status-blind eligibility.

Housing Assistance & Subsidies

‚ñº
Data Risk High Impact

Data Collected

  • Current residential address (Section 8, public housing)
  • Household member immigration status
  • Income verification documents
  • Landlord and property information
Risk Level: HIGH

HUD programs under Trump administration pressured to share data. ICE reportedly accessed U.S. Postal Service change-of-address databases. Mixed-status families face particular vulnerability - citizen children eligible but undocumented parents must be identified.

Documented Impacts

Immigrants avoided applying for housing assistance due to fears that providing address information could facilitate ICE raids at their homes.

Healthcare Programs (Medicaid, CHIP, FQHCs)

‚ñº
Data Risk Strong Protections

Privacy Framework

  • HIPAA protections for health information
  • Medicaid data generally prohibited from immigration enforcement use
  • FQHCs have confidentiality safeguards
Risk Level: MEDIUM (Despite Protections)

Trump administration DHS aggressively sought access to Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services data. State officials alarmed at federal requests for state Medicaid participant information despite privacy laws.

Oregon Progress

  • Cover All Kids (2017): OHP coverage for all children under 19, regardless of status (state-funded)
  • Cover All People: Phasing in Medicaid-like coverage for adult immigrants excluded from federal Medicaid, starting with 55+

Why This Works: State funding eliminates federal reporting requirements, reducing PII flow to federal systems while maintaining healthcare access.

Food & Nutrition Assistance (SNAP, WIC)

‚ñº
Data Risk High Impact

The Paradox

SNAP agencies generally barred from using applicant data for anything other than eligibility determination, BUT Trump administration DHS requested access to SNAP databases for immigration enforcement.

Documented Drop-Off
  • California: "Mass exodus from CalFresh" following federal policy changes
  • 10% decrease in food assistance participation post-2016 election
  • National: Over 15% of adults in immigrant families avoided non-cash benefits (2019)
  • Low-income immigrant families: 26% avoidance rate

Public Charge Rule Effect

Even programs NOT covered by public charge rule (like WIC, children's health insurance) saw significant drop-off due to widespread confusion and fear.

Sanctuary Policy Impact

Living in jurisdiction with sanctuary policies: 21% higher odds of SNAP enrollment (adjusted OR 1.21, 95% CI=1.11-1.31)

Most immigrant-friendly states: 16% higher enrollment odds (aOR=1.16, 95%CI=1.06-1.28)

Both protections together: 26% higher SNAP participation

Digital Surveillance: The Data Dragnet

üö® CRITICAL THREAT: ICE created a "vast web of data" by tapping federal, state, and commercial databases. Investigation found ICE collected driver location data for 3 in 4 Americans and utility records for about 75% of U.S. adults through private data brokers.

The Databases ICE Accessed

State Driver's License Data (DMV)

‚ñº
Data Breach Critical

The Nlets Backdoor

Little-known network (Nlets) allowed inter-agency law enforcement data exchange. ICE and Homeland Security Investigations ran nearly 900,000 queries to state DMV databases in just one year.

Oregon Was Affected

Oregon officials only recently learned ICE had access to their DMV data and moved to cut it off. Most states were initially unaware they were "vacuuming up" residents' data for ICE.

What ICE Accessed

  • Home addresses
  • Photos (facial recognition scans)
  • Vehicle information
  • License status and history

The Risk: Immigrants who obtained licenses under confidentiality promises still had information end up in ICE's hands through automated queries.

Federal Agency Cross-Mining

‚ñº
Data Breach DOGE Initiative

Agencies Targeted by ICE/DHS

  • IRS: Taxpayer information of immigrants with removal orders
  • Social Security Administration: SSN records, disability benefits
  • Centers for Medicare & Medicaid: Health coverage data
  • SNAP Administration: Food stamp recipient lists
  • HUD: Public housing tenant information
  • Veterans Affairs: Benefits recipient data
  • Department of Education: Student records
  • U.S. Postal Service: Change of address databases

IRS-ICE Memorandum of Understanding (MOU)

Secret agreement revealed in 2025: Treasury Department shares taxpayer information with DHS. Officials labeled undocumented individuals with removal orders as "under criminal investigation" to circumvent tax confidentiality laws.

Impact: Undocumented taxpayers who paid taxes with assurances of privacy now face deportation risk from their tax data.

California State Program Subpoena

ICE subpoenaed California program aiding elderly immigrants (ineligible for federal SSI) to obtain participant list - showing even state-funded programs not immune to federal demands.

SAVE System & Cross-Agency Databases

‚ñº
Data System Verification

Systematic Alien Verification for Entitlements (SAVE)

Federal system used by states to verify immigration status for benefit eligibility. Under Trump, system "overhauled" into streamlined platform giving officials at all levels faster, centralized status checks.

The Concern: Immigrants worried SAVE check could flag information to ICE if they weren't eligible. System creates centralized database of status information linked to benefit applications.

AI-Powered Surveillance: Palantir's ImmigrationOS

‚ñº
AI System Critical

The Master Database

ICE expanded contract with data-mining firm Palantir to build "ImmigrationOS" - a master database using AI to:

  • Sift through records from multiple agencies
  • Track immigrants in near-real-time
  • Correlate data across systems
  • Coordinate deportation logistics
Sweeping Integration

Any personal detail handed to one government entity could be correlated with others. Example: Filing taxes with ITIN or applying for child's health insurance provides address/household information indexed in ICE system.

Private Data Brokers & "Data Exhaust"

‚ñº
Commercial Pervasive

What ICE Purchases

Georgetown Law investigation found ICE collected from enormous range of sources:

  • Driver location data: For 3 in 4 Americans
  • Utility records: About 75% of U.S. adults
  • Phone bills
  • Electricity accounts
  • License plate scans
"Data Exhaust" Trails

Create risk even for immigrants who avoid direct government contact. Everyday activities (driving, paying bills, using cellphone) generate records ICE can buy or access.

Personal information once siloed for specific uses became increasingly interoperable across agencies during Trump era.

Result:

Heightened likelihood of detentions + Amplified fear = Service avoidance

Community-Based Protection Strategies: What Works

üéØ CRITICAL FINDING

Effective protective strategies require multi-component approaches combining policy change, community education, institutional capacity-building, and adequate resources ‚Äî tailored to specific local enforcement patterns and community needs rather than uniform implementation of single interventions.

Strategy Categories with Evidence

1. Legal Strategies

‚ñº
Legal High Priority

Know-Your-Rights Training

  • Educational workshops
  • Distribution of red cards (rights reminder cards)
  • Community presentations

Limitation: Information alone insufficient to overcome fear when not coupled with broader institutional changes.

Legal Clinics & Representation

Quantitative Evidence: Legal aid focused on immigration hearings provided by EOIR-registered organizations showed particularly strong associations with protective outcomes:
  • More than 2-fold increase in sheriff office adoption of anti-detainer policies
  • 24% decrease in immigration background checks with each additional EOIR organization in a county

Power of Attorney Forms

Direct assistance with legal paperwork to ensure care for U.S.-born children in case of parental deportation.

Policy Advocacy & Litigation Support

  • Multi-state lawsuits to block harmful federal rules
  • Court settlements (e.g., Gonzalez v. ICE barring ICE from relying on flawed database information)
  • Oregon AG's challenges to public charge rule, information-sharing overreaches

2. Programmatic Strategies

‚ñº
Program Design High Priority

Modified Intake Procedures

  • Accepting various forms of ID: Not requiring specific government-issued documents
  • Delayed ID requirements: Not requiring identification until AFTER medical services provided
  • Avoiding home address documentation: Using alternative location indicators when possible
  • Data minimization: Collecting only strictly necessary information

Alternative Documentation

Accepting alternative proofs of residency, income, identity that don't create federal database trails.

Mobile Services & Co-Location

Bringing programs directly to immigrant communities, reducing geographic barriers exacerbated by enforcement concerns.

Virtual Service Delivery

COVID-19 Acceleration: Created both opportunities (reduced in-person exposure) and barriers (internet connectivity, device availability, digital literacy).

Most effective: When coupled with direct outreach and support rather than standalone.

Safe-Zone School Policies (Evidence-Based Success)

Multi-component approach:

  • Restricting physical access by immigration authorities to school premises
  • Creating resource centers and information centers for immigrant students/families
  • Training school staff to implement policies advancing students' academic opportunities
  • Allocating funds for supportive initiatives
  • Providing counseling on immigration-related issues

Quantitative Results: Strengthened children's focus, effort, expectations, parental involvement, and relationships. Specifically helped mitigate negative effects of immigration enforcement on educational performance.

7. Sanctuary & Firewall Policies

‚ñº
Sanctuary Critical

Sanctuary Policy Framework

Drawing bright line between local services and federal immigration enforcement:

  • Bans on sharing sensitive personal data with ICE without court order
  • Prohibitions on asking about immigration status in first place
  • Law enforcement non-cooperation: Not honoring ICE detainers without warrant
  • Agency-wide policies: Extending to civilian agencies, schools, hospitals, social services

Examples

  • Illinois: DMV barred from sharing license information with ICE absent judicial warrant/subpoena
  • NYC: Executive orders instructing public agencies not to disclose immigration status to federal authorities except where required by law
  • California: AB 699 (schools cannot collect immigration status info), SB 54 (local police/jails broadly prohibited from assisting ICE)

Oregon Opportunity

Build on sanctuary tradition by extending similar protections to state-funded programs. Policy that state agencies will not share client information for immigration enforcement purposes.

Implementation Critical

Policies that aren't well-publicized or understood by immigrant communities cannot influence behavior. Awareness of sanctuary protections was "the exception rather than the rule, particularly among immigrant men."

Sanctuary policies serve trust-building signaling functions but are insufficient alone - local police practices could undermine policy goals even where formal non-cooperation policies existed.

Factors Contributing to Strategy Success

  • Comprehensive immigration reform at policy level
  • Community engagement and dialogue
  • Organizational capacity to adapt intake procedures and ensure confidentiality
  • Addressing linguistic and cultural barriers
  • Multi-level alignment: Federal, state, local policy coordination
  • Adequate implementation resources
  • Champions and informal leaders (though not sufficient for sustainability alone)
  • Supportive national policy climate
  • Communication among personnel
  • Community partnerships
  • Shared mission for equity

Sustainability Challenges

Implementation Barriers Identified

  • Technology access disparities (internet, devices, digital literacy)
  • Resource limitations constraining capacity to meet increased demand
  • Ongoing need for adaptation to changing policy landscapes
  • Limited funding for culturally responsive programming
  • Local resistance to inclusionary policies
  • Federal policy threats undermining state efforts
  • Perception of legal complexity creating barriers to systemic strategies

Oregon-Specific Actions: What We Can Do Now

üéØ Oregon's Unique Position

Oregon has longstanding sanctuary law (1987, updated 2021) and progressive leadership, but gaps remain. Immigration arrests still tripled after 2017, and Washington County's 2025 emergency declaration shows ongoing crisis.

Immediate Actions for CBOs

1. Data Minimization & Safer Intake

‚ñº
Immediate Data Protection

Audit Current Practices

  • Review all intake forms - remove questions about immigration status unless ABSOLUTELY required
  • Collect only data strictly necessary to deliver service
  • Store data only as long as needed
  • Design forms so undocumented family members can be left unidentified if not seeking benefits themselves

Redesign Intake Processes

  • Accept various forms of identification
  • Don't require ID until after services provided (when possible)
  • Allow self-attestation of Oregon residency instead of demanding utility bills
  • Avoid documenting home addresses when alternative location indicators sufficient

Communication with Clients

  • Update intake scripts to clarify what information will/won't be shared
  • Post signs/flyers: "We won't report anything you tell us to immigration authorities - it's confidential"
  • Transparently warn clients about what information might be shared and what will be kept confidential
  • Dispel public charge myths with multilingual factsheets

2. Strengthen Internal Protocols

‚ñº
Immediate Operations

Develop ICE Response Protocol

  • Designate point person (executive director or lawyer) to handle law enforcement inquiries
  • Insist on proper legal documents (warrants, subpoenas) before considering compliance
  • Know organization's rights - role-play scenarios with staff
  • Post internal policy: "Do not hand over client information without director approval"

Data Security Measures

  • Encrypt sensitive client records
  • Store immigration-related data separately from other information
  • Implement data retention limits
  • Create audit trail of who accesses sensitive data

Staff Training

  • Train reception staff to be welcoming and not accidentally share information
  • Educate all staff on Oregon sanctuary law (SB 1543)
  • Know-your-rights training for staff to educate clients
  • Cultural competency and trauma-informed care training
